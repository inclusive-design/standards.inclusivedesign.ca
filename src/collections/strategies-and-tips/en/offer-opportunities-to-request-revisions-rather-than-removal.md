title: Offer opportunities to request revisions rather than removal
uuid: '09758457'
draft: false
strategies: |-
  - If a standard is outdated but still useful, let affected communities suggest updates or improvements instead of withdrawing it completely.
  - Provide clear instructions on how to request revisions for standards and what kind of changes can be made.
  - Use multiple accessible channels for submitting revision requests, such as online forms, email, phone, or mail.
  - Share outcomes of revision requests publicly to show how input influenced the standard.
  - Track and report revisions to show continuous improvement and maintain trust with communities.

examples: |-
  - \*\*In Sweden, [**SIS (Swedish Institute for Standards)**](https://www.sis.se/en/) \*\*includes public comment periods in the review of aging standards. This allows communities, including those historically excluded, to weigh in on whether a standard should be updated, maintained, or retired based on its ongoing social utility.
  - \*\*In the United Kingdom, the [**British Standards Institution (BSI)**](https://www.bsigroup.com/en-CA/) \*\*includes a public consultation step before withdrawing key standards. They notify affected communities, including disability organizations, and publish justifications for proposed withdrawals. This allows for feedback on **potential social or accessibility impacts before a final decision is made.**
  - \*\*In South Africa, \*\*[**SABS** ](https://www.sabs.co.za/)involves civil society groups through stakeholder forums where standard withdrawal proposals are discussed. These forums ensure that people impacted by the standards, including disabled and Deaf communities, can voice concerns or highlight continued relevance before withdrawal.
  - \*\*In the Netherlands, [**NEN**](https://www.nen.nl/en) \*\*uses online platforms to post withdrawal notices and collect public comments. If significant objections are raised, especially from equity-denied groups, the process is paused and reviewed further, ensuring greater accountability.
  - \*\*In Germany, [**DIN**](https://www.din.de/en) \*\*labels withdrawal announcements as “proposals” and opens them for a 60-day public consultation. The format includes reasons for withdrawal and invites input from user communities, including those with accessibility concerns.
  - \*\*In India, the \*\*[**Bureau of Indian Standards (BIS)**](https://www.bis.gov.in/?lang=en) has started using online portals where draft withdrawal notices are posted for public viewing. Users can submit objections or suggest revisions, and BIS outlines how such feedback may influence the final decision.
  - \*\*In Norway, [**Standards Norway**](https://standard.no/en/) \*\*uses plain language in withdrawal notices and publicly commits to reviewing input before finalizing the decision. They also send notices to relevant community groups to ensure those most impacted have a chance to respond.
